The Challenge

Wealth inequality is at 1920 levels! Statistics and stories abound showing how inequality has been rising, in terms of income, in terms of wealth, and in terms of opportunities.

Up to now we have seen only two ways to reduce inequality:

  • Revolutions. Terribly destructive, they often lead to totalitarian regimes rather than better outcomes, they are in fact the ultimate consequence of failure to address inequalities not a desirable solution.
  • Taxes. They are hard to optimize, lead to capital flight, create uneconomic behaviors and mostly feed bureaucracies rather than people. France has the highest overall taxes in the OECD but saw the greatest increase in its billionaires’ wealth in 2019 in parallel with an explosion of social unrest.

Looking at the top billionaires of the world today, and at most of the richest people of the last century, the value of their companies has been the driver of that wealth, and it is only because success was not shared more fairly that inequalities have been rising so much. The fairness of sharing is at the core of a possible mean to reduce inequalities.

Are the ultra-rich taking the right proportion of the wealth from a business they build or lead? Is the gain in wealth of a company justly shared with all company members or just a few? Is the contribution of all employees in a business properly recognized?

How important for the success of the company is the Mickey Mouse or Princess Jasmine greeting visitors at the entrance of parks? How crucial is the floor cleaner in Amazon’s warehouse? How does the receptionist at Google influences the wellbeing of employees which then translates in better productivity?

Stories abound about unfair executive rewards, grossly unrelated to performance, with abuses so large that anyone with their right mind sees they do not make sense, that they do not reflect any business logic nor build value for shareholders.

Meanwhile, salary increases for employees are important and often justified but history shows us that they just don’t make a dent in inequalities. Moreover, higher salaries often saddle a company with a long-term burden that make it defenseless against new competitors (the car industry or airlines are a good example where newcomers without legacy costs have an advantage).

As capital does fuels wealth and is justly rewarded in return. Capital is the tool to be used but capitalism is only fair and sustainable when benefits apply to all stakeholders.
