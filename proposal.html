Proposal

1% of capital distributed in shares every year equally among all employees

A 1% per year in company shares equally for all will have a significant positive impact and one that has a reasonable relationship with average salaries as we can see from this small sample below:

For each company in the table, we have divided 1% of their market cap by the number of their employees, giving us in the last column what each employee would receive when given equally 1% of the company. We also indicate the average employee yearly salary in the last column to give an idea of the impact of the 1% yearly distribution compared to this average.

Averages are of course simplifications, but the table shows that paying out 1% equally to each is both reasonable compared to the average salary and is also significant. Clearly this would have a profound impact for most employees without being excessive at all. Furthermore the 1% is also reasonable from an investor viewpoint when the average movement of the SP500 over the past 10 years is 1% daily, meaning that such annual capital distribution is around the average day of share price movement so this solution would not affect disproportionally shareholders. The cost to investors is minimal but the impact to each employee is significant.

THE IMPACT FOR WORKERS

Whilst it is easy to talk about the beneficial impact for workers, whether it is money spent on a new car, or a deposit on a house, or putting the received shares aside for the kids’ college or their retirement, the psychological impact would be equally as big as the financial one.

Sharing 1% equally between all company workers sends a strong message that everyone is important in the company, strengthening its culture and making everyone ambassadors for the success of the company.

THE IMPACT FOR COMPANIES

Business is well aware of the importance of culture and for every employee to feel they are an important part of the success. Greater fairness in the sharing of a company’s success is a powerful tool to achieve these objectives.

Whilst many other benefits are not linked to the performance of a company and may therefore happen at the wrong time, the cost of this 1% benefit also does not impose a long-term burden on the company’s operations or profitability, but is well aligned with the financial health of the company at the time it is granted.

The risk of a transfer of too much power away from management is also extremely limited: the considerable number of employees, employee turnover, sales of their shares by employees and buy-back or new issuance by the companies will keep employee ownership very small for decades and decades. On the contrary, employees owners could contribute more effectively as stable shareholders than some hedge funds that today have great powers but are not stable backers and have a very short term outlook. It is a win for all.
